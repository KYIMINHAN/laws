Dear all, I just looked in on the Philadelphia court system concerning the qualifications of jurors. As it happens, I was called to report to jury duty this past Winter, and was questioned by a judge (chiefly because I reject the death penalty). In any case, not responding to a call to jury duty is a punishable offense under Pennsylvania law. So, I did my duty, I showed up, and I was qualified to serve. The judge, and both the prosecutor and the defense lawyer can object to particular possible jurors and keep them from being empaneled. In consequence, the actual trial is proceeded by a process of jury selection, in which all parties to the case are present. Here is the list of qualifications for members of a jury in Philadelphia (where trial by jury has been practiced for hundreds of years--since the 17th century, I would think): In order to qualify for jury service, you must meet the following requirements: You must be able to read, write, and speak the English language. You must not have been convicted of a crime punishable by imprisonment for more than one year. You must be physically and mentally able to perform the functions of a juror. You must be a United States citizen at the time you are summoned. You must be a Philadelphia resident and at least 18 years of age. If you are not mentally or physically able to serve as a juror, provide medical certification in the remarks section of the questionnaire. If you are requesting to be excused for a job related reason, your employer should certify the hardship in the remarks section.

I wonder about the argument apparently made against the use of the jury. Various countries have differing traditions, and may be more or less democratic; but in any case, India apparently used to make wider use of trial by jury, according to your account of the matter. When did this change? Is it a quite recent change, or did it take place some decades back? In the usual case, the judge is charged with instructing the jury about the law, so that their initial possible ignorance of the law is attended to in that way. But if initial ignorance of the law and the danger of the jury being "guided by emotions" compels rejection of a popular voice in trials, then it would seem that the same argument would be used to reject a popular voice in the election of state officials. Why would not rejection of the democratic element in the justice system carry over to rejection of the democratic selection of officials? Edit Question Answer

In 2004, the Law School of my University had the privilege of receiving the visit of Ruth Bader Ginsburg, the oldest US Supreme Court Justice in office right now, and a splendid human being who is quite knowledgeable of literature and the fine arts. We had lunch under a beautiful tent in the interior patio of one of the oldest buildings in Old San Juan amidst the first Triennial of Puerto Rican and Caribbean Art, an international affair Ginsburg walked through with much joy and quite a lot of comments. Being a Chancellor's aid in academic affairs, I had the chance to be present there. After a long conversation with judges and lawyers and law students avid to meet and listen and dialogue with Ginsburg, I had the chance to make a candid question: "As a lawyer, you had the chance to move the US Supreme Court to sketch new legislation beneficial to women. What have you been able to do from the highest bench in furtherance of women rights?" Her answer was completely unexpected. She flashed a generous smile and said: "We US Supreme Court Justices do not make legislation, we just adjudicate according to existing law, we do not invent, we find in favor of the best and fairest proposition. Thus, it is the lawyer's duty to bring to the court the best argument grounded on the best of reasons, especially if the present state of law is shown to be unfair to a community of citizens. Let the lawyers demand justice, and we will give it to them even if we have to strike down an existing law and pronounce it unconstitutional. And then let the lawmakers do their work and make the necessary amendments or new legislation."
